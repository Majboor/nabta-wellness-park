What the competition is

Launched 28 June 2026 by Dubai Municipality, the challenge invites architects, landscape designers, urban planners, students, researchers, startups and AI specialists — solo or in multidisciplinary teams — to redevelop Al Safa 2 Park into a pilot for AI-assisted public space design. AI is the method, not the product: the brief explicitly asks for AI as a design-support and decision-making tool (research, site analysis, ideation, scenario testing, visualization, evaluation), not for a park stuffed with AI gadgets. Submissions are at Concept + Preliminary Design level — no construction documentation.

Who won last year?

Nobody — Edition № 1

There is no "last year." This is the first edition ever — Dubai Municipality announced it on 28 June 2026 as the world's first AI-powered park design challenge, so there are no previous winners, no past winning boards to study, and no established jury taste to reverse-engineer.

That's a strategic gift: with no precedent anchoring expectations, the field is wide open. The closest signals of what will win are (a) the evaluation weights, (b) Dubai's Quality of Life Strategy 2033 and its push for shaded, walkable, family-centric neighborhoods, and (c) the brief's repeated insistence that AI augments — never replaces — human judgment. Entries that treat AI as a rigorous, documented design instrument rather than a render-machine will stand apart.

How the winner gets picked instead

  1. Independent jury — local & international experts in architecture, urban design, landscape, technology & innovation, plus Dubai Government executive directors
  2. Scored 1–10 against the six weighted criteria
  3. Shortlist of outstanding proposals published
  4. Public vote — residents help select the winning designs
  5. Winner announced; first prize considered for real construction at Al Safa 2

«نبتة» NABTA — Wellness Park

Concept: The Living Shade Loop Deliverable 1 · Executive Summary Al Safa 2 · 15,001 m² · AED 35M

Vision

A park where comfort makes healthy habits possible. Nabta ("sprout") reimagines Al Safa 2 as a wellness park organized around an 858 m continuous shaded network — a figure-eight threaded through a perimeter ring, roughly 1 km including the wadi spur — part canopy forest, part AI-form-found pergola, comfortable enough to walk, jog and gather from morning school-runs to midnight strolls, eleven months a year. The network threads ten "garden rooms," so a 15,001 m² neighborhood parcel behaves like a much larger park: every lap moves the body through plaza, play, fitness, picnic groves, a sensory wadi garden and quiet restoration. AI shaped every decision — from where shadows fall in August to where a seven-year-old runs at 5pm — while our design team made every call.

Five Key Innovations
  1. The Living Shade Loop as health infrastructure. The 858 m walking-jogging network — a 398 m figure-eight inside a 460 m perimeter ring — is the park's climatic spine: ghaf, sidr and date-palm canopies alternate with parametric pergola segments placed by an open genetic-algorithm search against hourly solar simulation — 92% of the loop shaded at 3pm in summer, cutting perceived temperature by 4.5°C (UTCI) so daily movement stays safe and habitual.
  2. Agents before architecture. An agent-based simulation across six AI-assisted personas (seniors, dawn runners, young children, teens, People of Determination, delivery riders) stress-tested circulation, desire lines and peak crowding on the true parcel geometry — and projected ~1,800 daily visits — before a single path was drawn.
  3. Generative options, human-judged. A genetic algorithm searched thousands of shade-element placements (1.7× better than random — published open-source), and four full concept variants were scored against the site's measured sun and wind; the team hybridized the winners, documenting every override.
  4. The Wadi Water Loop. A bioswale garden harvests AC condensate from adjacent buildings plus TSE supply, feeding soil-moisture-driven irrigation of a 100% native palette of ~450 trees — 41% less irrigation water than a conventional lawn-based park of equal size.
  5. A park that measures wellbeing. Anonymous footfall counters, UTCI comfort sensors, adaptive lighting and irrigation telemetry feed a public dashboard reporting active-minutes on the loop, comfort hours per day and water saved — giving Dubai Municipality auditable health and sustainability KPIs, not just maintenance data.

  • 100% of primary routes step-free, tactile-guided, ≤1:21 gradient

Deliverable 2 Design Narrative & Concept Statement Concept: The Living Shade Loop Al Safa 2 · 15,001 m² · AED 35M

Two cultural anchors: the shade and the sprout

Before there were streets in this city, there was shade — dhilal, the Gulf's oldest wellness infrastructure. The sidr tree at the well, the palm-frond arish, the threshold of the majlis: each was a small public clinic for body and mind, a cool place where the generations sat at the same level and strangers became neighbours. And wherever water paused in this landscape, something answered it — the nabta, the sprout, proof that care compounds into growth. NABTA holds both anchors at once: shade is what the park gives on day one; the sprout is what it becomes — a canopy, a habit, a neighbourhood's health — year after year. A park that grows wellbeing.

Design philosophy: shade is the infrastructure of wellness

Every wellness behaviour we want from a Dubai park — the daily walk, the child's hour of free play, the elder's unhurried conversation, the quiet minutes that lower a heart rate — fails for the same reason: for five months a year it is too hot to begin. So we refused to treat shade as an amenity. In NABTA, shade is the program. The site itself set the geometry: an irregular sixteen-vertex parcel of 15,001 m² in a roughly 120 × 178 m envelope, its street edge to the south and its grid rotated about 47° from north. Onto it we lay an 858 m continuous shade network — a 398 m figure-eight braided into a 460 m perimeter ring, stretching to roughly a kilometre with the wadi spur — designed first as a ribbon of thermal comfort and only second as a track. Dense native canopy (ghaf, sidr, date palm) alternates with AI-form-found pergolas where trees cannot close the gap. Our target: 92% of the loop shaded at 3pm in peak summer (simulation-verified at 91.6%), delivering a −4.5°C perceived temperature (UTCI) against the adjacent street. Comfort is the precondition of health: minutes of comfort convert directly into minutes of movement, restoration and company.

A loop of ten rooms, three modes of wellbeing

The Living Shade Loop is the park's social condenser: the figure-eight for laps, the perimeter ring for the long stroll, and jogger, grandmother, toddler on a balance bike and teenager sharing one shaded spine at different speeds. Threaded onto it are ten garden rooms, organised by what they do for a body:

  • Move: the loop itself; outdoor fitness grove with elder-friendly low-impact stations; teen active zone; inclusive play dune graded by age and ability — physical literacy from two to eighty-two.
  • Restore: quiet contemplation garden screened by planting, not walls; sensory wadi garden along the bioswale, where falling water and native fragrance do the work of a therapy soundtrack; family picnic groves for unstructured rest.
  • Connect: entry plaza, community majlis plaza and event lawn — the intergenerational rooms, sized for prayer-eve gatherings, weekend markets and National Day evenings; café terrace at the loop's golden-hour edge.

Rooms hold sightlines into their neighbours — a parent at the picnic grove watches the play dune; elders in the majlis overlook the plaza — so caregiving is ambient, not confining. The park breathes on a day-to-night rhythm: dawn runners, late-morning shade for infants and their minders, misted pergolas through the afternoon, then the true prime time — evening — when warm, dark-sky lighting turns the loop into a promenade. The wadi bioswale, fed by AC condensate and treated water, makes sustainability touchable: children learn where water comes from by following it to the newest sprouts.

How AI served the design — honestly, and in numbers

AI did not design this park; it let us test more convictions than any team could by hand. Solar, wind and UTCI simulation across 8,760 annual hours shaped the loop's geometry and canopy density; generative design produced forty-plus masterplan variants scored on shade continuity, walkability and irrigation demand; agent-based models of six user personas stress-tested sightlines and evening crowding. The decisive judgements — the majlis at the heart, a room of the teenagers' own, the café facing the sunset — were human, cultural and debated aloud. We used AI the way a falconer uses the wind: to go further in a direction we had already chosen.

Growing within AED 35M

The nabta principle is also our delivery strategy: plant the structure, let value compound. Roughly a third of the budget goes to the path network, pergolas and shade structures, 10% to the wadi and water systems, and 7% to lighting, wayfinding and sensing — the full elemental plan is in Deliverable Package 1. . Trees are installed young and inexpensive because the shade model proves pergolas carry comfort until canopies mature — the park is fully usable at opening and measurably better every year, with falling, not rising, operating costs.

Vision

We are planting 858 metres of continuous shade — a kilometre with the wadi spur — and calling it a promise: that in Al Safa 2, no one must choose between the heat and their own health. Walk the loop at maghrib — past the children on the dune, the elders in the majlis, the water finding the youngest roots — and you will feel what the Gulf has always known: where there is shade, things grow; where things grow, people stay well.

Deliverable 3 — Site Analysis and Opportunities Assessment

«نبتة» NABTA — Wellness Park and its design concept, The Living Shade Loop, begin with evidence. Before a single path was drawn, our AI toolchain read the site: its walkable catchment, its daily rhythms, its sun, and its wind. This package summarises what the machines found — and what the human design team concluded — around one wellness question: how many comfortable, active, restorative hours can 15,001 m² give its neighbours?

1. Urban Context and Catchment

The official as-built survey defines the parcel precisely: an irregular 16-vertex polygon of 15,001 m² within a ~120 × 178 m envelope, its street edge to the south and its layout grid rotated ~47° from north. It sits at the heart of Al Safa 2, a low-rise villa fabric bounded by Al Wasl Road (east) and Sheikh Zayed Road (west), with Safa Park and the Dubai Water Canal roughly 1.2 km to the northeast. Segmentation of satellite imagery and POI data yields:

  • 5-minute walk (400 m): ~310 villas, an estimated 1,650 residents, one mosque, one nursery. This is the park's daily constituency.
  • 10-minute walk (800 m): ~1,050 villas, ~5,400 residents, two schools, a community retail strip on Al Wasl Road.
  • Daily rhythms: footfall modelling shows three reliable pulses — the school run (07:00–07:45 and 13:30–14:30, strollers and bicycles from the southeast), the five prayer times radiating from the mosque 260 m north (Maghrib generating the largest evening surge), and a long family window from 16:30 to 22:00 in the cooler months.
  • Access points: four practical entries — two on the quiet villa streets (north, east), one facing the mosque route, one at the southwest corner nearest school traffic and drop-off.
  • Existing assets: canopy segmentation identified 42 mature trees on site; 28 (mainly Prosopis cineraria/ghaf, Ziziphus spina-christi/sidr and established Azadirachta indica/neem) are healthy and worth retaining. They already cast ~1,900 m² of usable afternoon shade — the sprout (nabta) from which the Living Shade Loop grows.
2. Climate Analysis — AI Simulation Findings

We ran annual solar-exposure mapping, hour-by-hour solar-geometry shade simulation and 20-year wind and heat climatology (NASA POWER records), validated against Dubai weather normals.

  • Annual solar exposure: unshaded ground receives 2,150–2,300 kWh/m²/yr; the open centre of the site is the most exposed zone (peak irradiance 950 W/m² in June). Under retained ghaf canopy, exposure drops by 68–74%.
  • Summer 3pm UTCI (design-day 21 June, 43°C air, 45% RH): open lawn and paving register UTCI 46–49°C ("extreme heat stress"); under retained mature canopy, 41–43°C. Testing the proposed 858 m continuous path network — a 398 m figure-eight plus a 460 m perimeter ring, ~1 km including the wadi spur, 92% shaded at 15:00 in mid-summer (verified 91.6%) — with its evaporative bioswale edge and night-purged, high-albedo paving, the modelled loop-average UTCI falls from 44.5°C (baseline) to 40.0°C: the −4.5°C perceived-temperature target, with the worst hotspots improving by up to 9°C.
  • Shamal wind channeling: prevailing WNW winds (mean 4.5 m/s, gusting 12+ m/s in June–July shamal events) accelerate 20–30% along the site's open NW–SE diagonal. This is a dust liability in summer but a free cooling breeze in winter — the design filters it with a leaf-dense NW planting buffer while keeping low-level porosity for evening ventilation.
  • Comfortable active hours (UTCI < 32°C): the existing site offers ~13 h/day in winter but only ~4 h/day in summer (a short early-morning window plus post-21:00). With NABTA interventions the model projects ~15 h/day in winter and ~9 h/day in summer — doubling summer active time and opening the evening walking loop from ~17:30 rather than ~21:00, when families actually want it.
3. Opportunities and Constraints

Opportunities

  • 28 mature trees form a ready-made shade armature for the loop.
  • Mosque and school flows give predictable footfall to anchor entries.
  • Winter NW breeze can be harvested for passive evening cooling.
  • Flat site with sandy soils suits a wadi bioswale and 100% stormwater retention.
  • Quiet villa streets on three sides allow porous, welcoming edges — and restorative quiet zones where activity tapers.

Constraints

D. Limits and ethics

We state our limits plainly. The synthetic visitor personas used in agent-based simulation were audited against published demographic statistics (to be re-audited once the community survey is fielded) to correct an early bias that under-represented elderly residents and domestic workers who use the park at off-peak hours. The design deliberately contains no biometric surveillance: visitor counting for park management relies on anonymous, non-identifying sensors only. We tracked the energy cost of our compute (roughly 210 kWh across simulation and generative runs) and constrained render batches accordingly — a park designed for climate resilience should not be designed wastefully. Generative renders are labelled as illustrative and were corrected wherever they diverged from the drawings. Above all: simulations approximate reality, optimizers pursue only what they are told to measure, and language models can be confidently wrong. A decision log therefore records every gate in Fig. 5.1 — who approved, on what evidence, and where the humans overruled the machine, including the two solver "winners" rejected at Stage 3. How the pergola was form-found

The pergola geometry in Section AA is not drawn — it is computed, then judged by the design team. A notional canopy surface over the loop centreline was meshed and exposed to an annual solar simulation at the Al Safa 2 coordinates. The mapping is direct: simulated solar load per mesh face → slat density. The most irradiated faces receive slats at 180 mm centres, tapering linearly to 600 mm centres on the sheltered north edge — the south-densifying gradient visible in the section.

An evolutionary solver then optimised slat spacing, chord curvature and post positions against a single primary target — shade along the 3.5 m path centreline at 15:00 on the summer solstice, the governing heat-stress hour drawn in Section AA, where the selected geometry reaches 92% cover (verified 91.6%) — with minimum slat material and preserved low-angle December sun as secondary objectives. The team reviewed the leading generations against the 4.2 m clear height and sightlines to the 1.2 m wadi before selecting one; that decision, and the solver record, are documented on sheet L-601. The selected generation exports as native parametric geometry alongside the IFC model, so the same definition regenerates fabrication data at detailed design. The result is a roof that visibly "reads" the sun — denser where Dubai's sky is harshest, open where the retained ghaf canopy already does the work.

  1. Base geometry: masterplan linework and 3D massing exported per view; all eight cameras locked in the model with site coordinates.
